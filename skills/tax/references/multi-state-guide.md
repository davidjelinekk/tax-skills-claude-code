# Multi-State Tax Guide for LLCs and Pass-Through Entities

## When Multi-State Filing Is Required

### Physical Nexus
- **Office, employees, or inventory** in another state creates filing obligations
- Even a single employee working remotely in another state can trigger nexus
- Temporary physical presence (e.g., trade shows) may create nexus depending on state rules

### Economic Nexus
- **Post-Wayfair (2018)**: States can assert sales tax nexus based on economic activity alone
- **Common sales tax thresholds**: $100K in revenue OR 200 transactions in the state
- **Income tax economic nexus**: varies by state — some use revenue thresholds, others use factor presence

### Factor Presence Nexus
- Some states assert income tax nexus based on exceeding thresholds in any apportionment factor:
  - Sales exceeding a dollar threshold (e.g., $500K+)
  - Payroll exceeding a dollar threshold
  - Property exceeding a dollar threshold
- **MTC factor presence nexus standard**: $50K property, $50K payroll, $500K sales, or 25% of total (many states adopt variations)

## Apportionment vs Allocation

### Apportionment
- Divides **business income** among states based on factors (sales, payroll, property)
- Most states now use **single-factor (sales only)** apportionment
- **IL uses single sales factor** for most businesses (35 ILCS 5/304)
- Three-factor formula (sales, payroll, property) still used by some states but increasingly rare

### Allocation
- Assigns **specific non-business items** (real property income, dividends from non-unitary entities) to specific states
- Real property income → allocated to state where property is located
- Interest/dividends → typically allocated to state of commercial domicile

### Sourcing Methods
- **Market-based sourcing** (where customer is): majority of states, including IL
- **Cost-of-performance** (where work is done): minority of states (e.g., some older rules)
- **IL uses market-based sourcing** for services — revenue sourced to where the customer receives the benefit

## Common Multi-State Scenarios for LLC Owners

### Remote Consulting for Out-of-State Clients
- Physical presence in client's state (travel for meetings) may create nexus depending on duration/frequency
- If all work performed remotely from IL, generally no nexus in client's state for income tax
- Some states (notably NY) have aggressive "convenience of the employer" rules

### SaaS Business with Customers in Multiple States
- **Sales tax nexus**: almost certainly triggered in states where economic nexus thresholds are met
- **Income tax nexus**: varies — factor presence and economic nexus rules differ by state
- Track revenue by customer state to monitor threshold crossings

### Employee in Another State
- Creates nexus in that state for both income tax and employment tax purposes
- Must register for withholding, unemployment insurance, and potentially other taxes in employee's state
- Even a single remote employee triggers filing obligations

### Selling Physical Products
- **Economic nexus for sales tax** (Wayfair): $100K revenue or 200 transactions in most states
- Must register, collect, and remit sales tax in each state where nexus exists
- Use sales tax automation software (e.g., Avalara, TaxJar) for multi-state compliance

## State-Specific Filing Requirements (Key States)

### New York
- Aggressive nexus assertion
- **Convenience-of-employer rule**: if employer is in NY and employee works remotely from another state for the employee's convenience (not employer's necessity), income taxed as if earned in NY
- LLC filing fee based on NY-source gross income ($25-$4,500)
- Must file IT-204 (partnership return) if NY-source income

### California
- **$800 annual LLC fee** (minimum, due regardless of income)
- **Gross receipts fee**: additional $900-$11,790 based on total revenue (not just CA revenue)
- Aggressive nexus — asserts economic nexus broadly
- Franchise Tax Board actively pursues out-of-state entities with CA customers
- Must file Form 568 (LLC return)

### Texas
- **No personal income tax** but **franchise tax (margin tax)** on entities
- Applies to entities with TX revenue over $2.47M (no-tax-due threshold)
- Rate: 0.375% (wholesale/retail) or 0.75% (other) on taxable margin
- Must file TX franchise tax report annually

### Florida
- **No personal income tax**
- Corporate income tax: **5.5%** on C-Corps (does not apply to pass-throughs unless they elect corporate taxation)
- Sales tax: 6% state + local surtax
- Favorable for pass-through entity owners

### States with No Income Tax
- **Alaska** (AK) — no state income or sales tax
- **Florida** (FL) — no personal income tax
- **Nevada** (NV) — no personal or corporate income tax; commerce tax on gross revenue >$4M
- **New Hampshire** (NH) — limited: no tax on wages; interest & dividends tax repealed 2025
- **South Dakota** (SD) — no personal or corporate income tax
- **Tennessee** (TN) — limited: no tax on wages; Hall tax on interest/dividends repealed 2021
- **Texas** (TX) — no personal income tax; franchise tax on entities
- **Washington** (WA) — no personal income tax; B&O tax on gross receipts
- **Wyoming** (WY) — no personal or corporate income tax

## IL Apportionment Schedule

### Filing Requirements
- **Form IL-1065, Schedule B** for multi-state partnerships/LLCs
- Required when LLC has income from sources both within and outside Illinois

### Sales Factor Calculation
- **Sales factor** = IL-sourced revenue / total revenue everywhere
- IL income = total business income × sales factor
- Only business income is apportioned; non-business income is allocated

### Throwback Rule
- **IL does NOT have a throwback rule** (unlike some states)
- Revenue from sales into states where the LLC has no nexus is "nowhere income" — not thrown back to IL
- This can be advantageous: income sourced to no-nexus states escapes IL taxation

### P.L. 86-272 Protection
- Federal law (P.L. 86-272) prevents states from taxing businesses whose **only activity** in the state is solicitation of orders for **tangible personal property**
- **Does NOT apply to**: services, digital goods, SaaS, licensing, or intangibles
- If LLC sells services or digital products, P.L. 86-272 provides no protection
- States are increasingly narrowing interpretations (e.g., CA, NY treat website activity as exceeding P.L. 86-272)

## Practical Guidance

### Revenue Tracking
- Track revenue by state/customer location from day one
- Maintain records sufficient to support apportionment calculations
- Use accounting software categories or tags for state-level reporting

### Monitoring Nexus Thresholds
- Review economic nexus thresholds quarterly in states where you have significant revenue
- Set up alerts when approaching $100K revenue in any state (sales tax)
- Monitor factor presence thresholds for income tax nexus

### Voluntary Disclosure Agreements (VDAs)
- If you discover unfiled obligations in a state, consider a **VDA** before the state contacts you
- VDAs typically offer: waiver of penalties, limited lookback period (3-4 years vs. unlimited)
- Available in most states through the Multistate Tax Commission or directly with state revenue departments

### De Minimis Activity
- Many states have de minimis exceptions, but they vary widely
- Some states exempt businesses with less than a threshold of sales, payroll, or property
- Do not assume de minimis protection — verify state-specific rules

### Multi-State Tax Software
- Consider dedicated multi-state tax software for compliance
- Services like Avalara (sales tax), TaxJar, or multi-state income tax preparation software
- Cost is often justified once filing in 3+ states

## PTE Tax in Other States

### Availability
- Many states now offer **PTE tax elections** similar to Illinois
- As of 2025, 30+ states have enacted PTE tax provisions
- Each state's election has its own rules, rates, and deadlines

### Multi-State PTE Coordination
- If LLC operates in multiple states, may elect PTE in **each state** that offers it
- Each state's PTE tax is calculated on income apportioned/allocated to that state
- Entity-level deduction applies for federal purposes in each electing state (per IRS Notice 2020-75)

### Credit Coordination
- Ensure credits for PTE taxes paid to other states are properly claimed on IL returns
- IL allows a credit for taxes paid to other states on the same income (IL-1040, Schedule CR)
- Avoid double taxation: coordinate PTE credits across all states where elections are made
- Document the credit calculations thoroughly for each state
