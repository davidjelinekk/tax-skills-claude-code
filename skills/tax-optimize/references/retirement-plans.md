## SEP IRA — Detail

### Contribution Limits (2025-2026)
- **2025**: 25% of compensation, max **$70,000**
- **2026**: 25% of compensation, max **$72,000**
- Compensation cap: $350,000 (2025), $360,000 (2026)

### For Self-Employed (Not S-Corp)
The "25% of net SE income" is actually an effective rate of ~20% due to the circular
calculation (you must reduce by 50% of SE tax first):

| Net SE Income | Effective Contribution |
|---------------|----------------------|
| $50,000 | ~$9,294 |
| $75,000 | ~$13,940 |
| $100,000 | ~$18,587 |
| $150,000 | ~$27,881 |
| $200,000 | ~$37,174 |
| $350,000+ | $70,000 (max) |

### For S-Corp Shareholder-Employees
- 25% of W-2 wages (not total distributions)
- At $100,000 salary: $25,000 SEP contribution

### Pros
- Simplest to set up and administer
- No annual filing requirements
- Flexible — contribute as much or as little as you want each year
- Deadline extends to filing deadline including extensions (Oct 15)

### Cons
- No employee deferral component (limits total at lower incomes)
- No Roth option
- No loan provision
- Must cover all eligible employees at the same percentage

## Solo 401(k) — Detail

### Contribution Limits (2025)
**Employee Deferral:**
- Under 50: **$23,500**
- Age 50-59, 64+: **$31,000** ($23,500 + $7,500 catch-up)
- Age 60-63: **$34,750** ($23,500 + $11,250 super catch-up)

**Employer Contribution:**
- 25% of W-2 wages (S-Corp) or ~20% of net SE income (sole prop)

**Total Maximum:**
- Under 50: **$70,000**
- Age 50-59, 64+: **$77,500**
- Age 60-63: **$81,250**

### 2026 Limits
- Employee deferral: **$24,500** (under 50)
- Catch-up (50-59, 64+): **$8,000** additional = $32,500
- Super catch-up (60-63): **$11,250** additional = $35,750
- Total max: **$72,000** (under 50), **$80,000** (50+), **$83,250** (60-63)

### Why Solo 401(k) Usually Beats SEP IRA

At $100,000 net SE income (sole prop, age 45):
- **SEP IRA**: ~$18,587 (employer only)
- **Solo 401(k)**: $23,500 deferral + ~$18,587 employer = **~$42,087**

At $60,000 net SE income (sole prop, age 45):
- **SEP IRA**: ~$11,152
- **Solo 401(k)**: $23,500 deferral + ~$11,152 employer = **~$34,652**

The employee deferral component is the key advantage — it adds $23,500+ regardless of income.

### Roth 401(k) Option
- Employee deferrals can go Roth (after-tax, tax-free growth)
- Employer contributions must be pre-tax (traditional)
- Strategy: Roth deferrals in low-income years, traditional in high-income years
- No income limit for Roth 401(k) contributions (unlike Roth IRA)

### Loan Provision
- Borrow up to $50,000 or 50% of vested balance (whichever is less)
- Must repay within 5 years (or 15 years for home purchase)
- Interest paid goes back into your own account
- No tax or penalty if repaid on time

### Key Deadlines
- **Plan establishment**: By December 31 of the tax year
- **Employee deferrals**: By December 31 (cannot be made retroactively)
- **Employer contributions**: By filing deadline including extensions
- **Form 5500-EZ**: When plan assets exceed $250,000 (due July 31)

### Setup
- Adopt a plan document (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard offer free plans)
- Obtain EIN for the plan (separate from business EIN)
- Open solo 401(k) account at chosen custodian

## Defined Benefit / Cash Balance Plans — Detail

### For High Earners (typically 45+)
These plans allow the largest possible tax-deductible contributions.

### Contribution Limits (Age-Based, 2025-2026)

| Age | Approximate Annual Contribution |
|-----|-------------------------------|
| 35 | ~$85,000-$95,000 |
| 40 | ~$120,000-$128,000 |
| 45 | ~$145,000-$155,000 |
| 50 | ~$197,000-$207,000 |
| 55 | ~$250,000-$265,000 |
| 60 | ~$325,000-$340,000 |
| 62 | ~$350,000-$359,000 |

### Combining DB + Solo 401(k)
- Can maintain BOTH plans simultaneously
- Solo 401(k) contributions are IN ADDITION to DB plan
- Total potential: $200K-$440K+ depending on age and income
- Requires sufficient business income to fund both

### Lifetime Benefit Limit
- **$3.7 million** lump sum equivalent (2026)
- Annual benefit at retirement: $290,000/year (2026)

### Requirements
- Annual actuarial certification ($1,500-$3,000/year)
- **Mandatory** minimum contributions in most years (cannot skip)
- Must be established by December 31 of the plan year
- Form 5500 filing required annually
- PBGC premiums may apply

### Best For
- High-income professionals age 45+ with consistent income
- Businesses with few or no employees (each employee increases cost)
- Owners wanting massive tax deductions in peak earning years
- Catch-up strategy for those behind on retirement savings

### Risks
- Mandatory contributions even in low-income years
- Complex administration
- Significant penalties for plan compliance failures
- Must fund for at least 3-5 years to avoid IRS scrutiny

## HSA as Stealth Retirement Account

### Triple Tax Advantage
1. Contributions are tax-deductible (above-the-line)
2. Growth is tax-free
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

### 2025-2026 Limits

| | 2025 | 2026 |
|---|------|------|
| Self-only | $4,300 | $4,400 |
| Family | $8,550 | $8,750 |
| Catch-up (55+) | +$1,000 | +$1,000 |

### Long-Term Strategy
- Pay current medical expenses out of pocket
- Let HSA compound (invest in index funds)
- After age 65: withdrawals for any purpose are penalty-free (taxed as ordinary income like Traditional IRA)
- Keep all medical receipts — can reimburse yourself from HSA years later (no deadline)

### OBBBA Change
- HSA-eligible bronze health plans now qualify for HSA contributions

## IRA Strategies

### Backdoor Roth IRA
- Available at any income level
- Step 1: Contribute to nondeductible Traditional IRA ($7,000 in 2025, $7,500 in 2026)
- Step 2: Convert to Roth IRA
- **Warning**: Pro-rata rule — if you have ANY pre-tax IRA money, the conversion is partially taxable
- **Fix**: Roll pre-tax IRA money into employer plan (Solo 401k) before converting

### Mega Backdoor Roth (if Solo 401k allows)
- Some Solo 401(k) plans allow after-tax employee contributions
- Can then do in-plan Roth conversion
- Allows up to ~$46,500 additional Roth contributions (2025)
- Requires specific plan provisions — check plan document

## 529 Plans (IL Bright Start)

### IL Tax Benefit
- State tax deduction: up to $10,000/individual, $20,000/couple per beneficiary per year
- At 4.95% IL rate = up to $495 (individual) / $990 (couple) per beneficiary

### Superfunding
- Contribute up to 5x annual gift exclusion at once: $95,000 (2025) per beneficiary
- Must elect to spread over 5 years on Form 709 (gift tax return)
- Couples can contribute $190,000 per beneficiary with gift-splitting

### Roth IRA Rollover (SECURE 2.0)
- 529 account must have been open 15+ years
- Subject to annual Roth IRA contribution limits
- Lifetime rollover cap: $35,000
- Contributions from last 5 years are ineligible
- Great for unused 529 funds

## Strategy by Income Level

| Net SE Income | Recommended Plan(s) |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Under $40K | Traditional/Roth IRA ($7,000) + HSA |
| $40K-$80K | Solo 401(k) + HSA + 529 |
| $80K-$150K | Solo 401(k) (max) + HSA + 529 + consider S-Corp |
| $150K-$300K | S-Corp + Solo 401(k) + HSA + consider DB plan if 45+ |
| $300K+ | S-Corp + DB/Cash Balance + Solo 401(k) + HSA + 529 |
