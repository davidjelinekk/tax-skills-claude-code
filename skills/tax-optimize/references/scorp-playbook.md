# S-Corp Operational Playbook — Maximizing Tax Benefits Post-Election

## Overview

This playbook covers strategies for **after** the S-Corp election is in place. For the
election decision itself (break-even analysis, reasonable salary, Form 2553), see
`entity-structure.md`. These are the operational tactics that extract maximum value from
the S-Corp structure.

---

## 1. Accountable Plan (The #1 S-Corp Strategy)

### What It Is
- A written plan allowing the S-Corp to reimburse the shareholder-employee for business
  expenses **tax-free**
- Reimbursements are: deductible by the S-Corp, NOT included in shareholder's W-2 income,
  NOT subject to payroll taxes
- Authority: IRC Section 62(c) and Reg. 1.62-2

### Requirements (All 3 Must Be Met)
1. **Business connection** — expenses must have a business purpose
2. **Adequate accounting** — must submit receipts/documentation within 60 days
3. **Return of excess** — must return any reimbursement exceeding actual expenses within 120 days

Failure to meet any requirement = reimbursements treated as wages (W-2 income + FICA).

### Reimbursable Expenses

- **Home office**: Business percentage of rent/mortgage, utilities, insurance, repairs,
  depreciation. The S-Corp reimburses the shareholder instead of the shareholder claiming
  Form 8829. This is BETTER than Form 8829 because it avoids depreciation recapture on
  home sale.
- **Vehicle**: Business mileage at IRS rate ($0.70/mile in 2025) or actual expenses.
  Shareholder submits mileage log to S-Corp.
- **Cell phone**: Business percentage of monthly plan
- **Internet**: Business percentage of monthly service
- **Travel, meals, entertainment**: Business travel expenses (meals still 50% deductible
  to the S-Corp)
- **Professional development**: Courses, certifications, books, conferences
- **Equipment**: Computer, printer, office furniture used at home
- **Health insurance**: For >2% shareholders, must be on W-2 (different from accountable
  plan — see Section 3 below)

### How to Set Up
1. Board resolution adopting the accountable plan (template: simple one-page resolution)
2. Written reimbursement policy document
3. Expense report form (date, description, business purpose, amount, receipt attached)
4. Regular submission schedule (monthly recommended)
5. Reimbursement via separate check or ACH from S-Corp to shareholder (NOT commingled
   with salary or distributions)

### Tax Savings Example

Shareholder has: home office (10% of $30,000/year home costs = $3,000), vehicle (12,000
business miles x $0.70 = $8,400), cell phone ($1,200/year x 70% = $840), internet
($1,200/year x 50% = $600).

| Item | Annual Reimbursement |
|------|---------------------|
| Home office | $3,000 |
| Vehicle | $8,400 |
| Cell phone | $840 |
| Internet | $600 |
| **Total** | **$12,840** |

Tax savings at 32% bracket: $12,840 x 32% = **$4,109** in income tax saved
Plus: no FICA on reimbursements (saves another 15.3% x $12,840 = **$1,965** if these
would otherwise have been salary)

**Total annual savings: ~$6,074**

### Comparison: Accountable Plan vs Form 8829 / Personal Deduction

| Method | Tax Benefit | Payroll Tax Impact | Depreciation Recapture Risk |
|--------|------------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| Accountable plan | S-Corp deduction, not on W-2 | No FICA | None (no home depreciation claimed) |
| Form 8829 (sole prop) | Schedule C deduction | Reduces SE income | Yes, on home sale |
| Itemize (W-2 employee) | Not available 2018-2025 (TCJA) | N/A | N/A |

### Audit Risk: LOW
- Low if properly documented with written plan, timely substantiation, and business purpose
- Higher risk if reimbursements are round numbers, undocumented, or excessive relative to
  business activity

## 2. Augusta Rule — Renting Home to S-Corp (Section 280A(g))

### How It Works
- You can rent your personal residence to your S-Corp for up to **14 days per year**
  without reporting the rental income
- The S-Corp deducts the rent as a business expense
- You receive the rent **tax-free** (not reported on your personal return)
- Must be for legitimate business use: board meetings, strategic planning sessions,
  partner meetings, company retreats, training sessions

### Requirements
- Rent must be at **fair market value** — document with comparable rental rates (VRBO,
  Airbnb comps for similar properties in your area)
- Must not exceed **14 days** per year
- Must be for a **bona fide business purpose** (documented agendas, meeting minutes,
  attendee lists)
- Payment must be by check or ACH (paper trail)
- Written rental agreement between you and the S-Corp

### FMV Determination
- Pull comparable rates from VRBO/Airbnb for similar homes in your area
- For full-home use: $500-$3,000/day depending on market and home size
- For partial-home use (just meeting space): proportionally less
- Document the comparables and keep them on file
- Consider getting a letter from a real estate professional

### Example
- Home FMV rental rate: $1,000/day (based on VRBO comps)
- 12 board meeting days per year: 12 x $1,000 = $12,000
- S-Corp deduction: $12,000 (reduces corporate taxable income)
- Your income: $0 (excluded under Section 280A(g))
- Tax savings at 32% bracket: $12,000 x 32% = **$3,840**

### Audit Risk: MODERATE
- IRS has not issued specific guidance challenging the Augusta Rule for S-Corps
- Tax Court has upheld the 14-day exclusion (Bolton v. Commissioner)
- Key risk factors: unreasonable FMV, no documented business purpose, exceeding 14 days
- Mitigation: conservative FMV, detailed meeting agendas/minutes, limit to 10-12 days
  for safety margin

## 3. S-Corp Fringe Benefits — >2% Shareholder Rules

### Health Insurance (>2% Shareholder)
- S-Corp pays (or reimburses) health insurance premiums for >2% shareholder-employees
- Premiums **MUST** be included on the shareholder's W-2 (Box 1, but NOT Boxes 3 or 5 —
  not subject to FICA)
- Shareholder then deducts the premiums on Form 1040, Schedule 1, Line 17
  (self-employed health insurance deduction)
- Net effect: deductible above-the-line, no FICA
- Applies to: medical, dental, vision, long-term care insurance
- Covers: shareholder, spouse, dependents, children under 27

### HSA Contributions
- S-Corp can make HSA contributions on behalf of >2% shareholders
- Must be included on W-2 (Box 1, not Boxes 3/5)
- Shareholder deducts on Form 1040 (above-the-line)
- Alternative: shareholder makes personal HSA contribution and deducts directly

### Other Fringe Benefits — Taxable to >2% Shareholders
These benefits are tax-free for regular employees but taxable (on W-2) for >2%
shareholders:
- Group term life insurance (normally $50K tax-free for employees)
- Meals and lodging for employer convenience (Section 119)
- Employee achievement awards
- De minimis fringe benefits (above $75)
- Transportation/parking benefits (normally $315/month tax-free)

### Benefits That ARE Tax-Free for >2% Shareholders
- Accountable plan reimbursements (Section 62(c)) — see Section 1 above
- Qualified retirement plan contributions (401k, SEP)
- Workers' compensation
- No-additional-cost services

## 4. Year-End Compensation Planning

### Salary vs Distribution Timing
S-Corp shareholders can adjust salary in Q4 to optimize:
- If income is higher than expected: increase salary to boost W-2 wages (increases 401k
  employer contribution room, increases QBI W-2 wage limit)
- If income is lower than expected: ensure minimum reasonable salary is met but don't
  overpay

### Year-End Bonus Strategy
- **Cash-basis S-Corp**: bonus must be PAID by Dec 31 to be deductible in current year
- **Accrual-basis S-Corp**: can accrue bonus by Dec 31 and pay within 2.5 months
  (by March 15) IF the shareholder-employee owns >50% — Section 267(a)(2) related party
  matching rule requires payment within 2.5 months
- Strategy: declare bonus in December to maximize current-year deductions, but ensure
  payroll taxes are properly calculated

### Payroll Tax Optimization
- Run final payroll in December that includes any year-end bonus
- Ensure W-2 reflects total reasonable compensation for the year
- Review: has Social Security wage base been reached? ($176,100 in 2025) If so, no
  additional SS tax on bonus.

## 5. Retirement Plan Contributions from S-Corp

### Solo 401(k) Through S-Corp
- Employee deferrals: up to $23,500 (2025) — must be withheld from payroll by Dec 31
- Employer contribution: up to 25% of W-2 wages — can be made until filing deadline
- Total max: $70,000 (2025)
- The S-Corp makes the employer contribution (deductible expense)
- Strategy: set salary to optimize both SE tax savings AND retirement contribution room

### SEP IRA Through S-Corp
- 25% of W-2 wages (not distributions)
- At $100,000 salary: $25,000 SEP
- Solo 401(k) is almost always better (employee deferral adds $23,500+)

### Optimizing Salary for Maximum Retirement Savings (2025)

Target: maximize Solo 401(k) total contribution of $70,000

- Employee deferral: $23,500
- Needed from employer: $70,000 - $23,500 = $46,500
- Required salary for 25% employer contribution: $46,500 / 0.25 = **$186,000**
- But SS wage base is $176,100, so you save FICA on excess

### The Salary / Retirement / SE Tax Triangle
Higher salary means:
- More SE tax (bad)
- More retirement contribution room (good)
- Lower QBI deduction (bad if below SSTB threshold)

Lower salary means:
- Less SE tax (good)
- Less retirement contribution room (bad)
- Higher QBI deduction (good if below SSTB threshold)

Find the optimum by modeling all three together.

## 6. S-Corp Distribution Planning

### Basis Tracking (Form 7203)
- Distributions in excess of basis are taxable as capital gains
- Track basis annually:
  - (+) Income allocated to shareholder
  - (+) Capital contributions
  - (-) Distributions
  - (-) Losses allocated to shareholder
  - (+) Loan repayments from corp
- **Debt basis**: only direct loans FROM shareholder TO S-Corp increase debt basis
  (not third-party loans or guarantees)

### Distribution Timing
- Take distributions AFTER ensuring adequate basis
- Year-end distributions: ensure they don't exceed accumulated basis
- If expecting a loss year: minimize distributions to preserve basis for loss deduction

### Reasonable Compensation First
- Always pay reasonable salary BEFORE taking distributions
- IRS can reclassify distributions as wages if salary is too low
- Document reasonable compensation analysis annually

## 7. S-Corp Startup Costs and Organizational Costs

### Startup Costs (Section 195)
- First $5,000: immediately deductible (reduced dollar-for-dollar for costs exceeding
  $50,000)
- Remainder: amortized over 180 months (15 years)
- Includes: market research, advertising before business opens, employee training, travel
  to find suppliers

### Organizational Costs (Section 248)
- First $5,000: immediately deductible (reduced dollar-for-dollar for costs exceeding
  $50,000)
- Remainder: amortized over 180 months
- Includes: legal fees for formation, state filing fees, accounting fees for setting up
  books
- Does NOT include: costs of selling stock, transfer taxes, commissions

## Combined Savings: Stacking S-Corp Strategies

Example for a single-owner S-Corp with $200,000 net profit, 32% marginal rate:

| Strategy | Annual Tax Savings |
|----------|-------------------|
| SE tax savings (salary $90K, distributions $110K) | ~$10,000 |
| Accountable plan reimbursements ($12,840) | ~$6,074 |
| Augusta Rule rental (12 days x $1,000) | ~$3,840 |
| Solo 401(k) contributions (tax deferral) | ~$22,400 deferred |
| Health insurance above-the-line deduction | ~$3,000-$8,000 |
| **Total direct annual savings** | **~$23,000+ (plus $22K deferred)** |

This is why S-Corp optimization is not just about the election — it's about running
the playbook after election.
