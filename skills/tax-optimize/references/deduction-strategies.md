# Deduction Strategies — QBI, Depreciation, SALT, Charitable, Credits

## QBI Deduction (Section 199A) — PERMANENT (OBBBA)

### Basics
- Deduct up to **20%** of Qualified Business Income from pass-through entities
- Reported on Form 8995 (simplified) or Form 8995-A (detailed)
- Made PERMANENT by OBBBA (was expiring 12/31/2025)

### Calculation
QBI Deduction = LESSER of:
1. 20% of QBI from all qualified businesses, OR
2. 20% of taxable income minus net capital gains

### Income Thresholds (2025)

| Filing Status | Full Deduction Below | Phase-out Range | Fully Phased Out |
|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Single | $191,950 | $191,950-$241,950 | $241,950 |
| MFJ | $383,900 | $383,900-$483,900 | $483,900 |

### SSTB Rules (Specified Service Trades or Businesses)
**SSTBs include**: health, law, accounting, actuarial, performing arts, consulting,
athletics, financial services, brokerage, investing/trading

**NOT SSTBs**: engineering, architecture

- Below threshold: full 20% regardless of business type
- In phase-out range: partial deduction using complex formula
- Above threshold: ZERO deduction for SSTBs

### Above-Threshold Non-SSTBs — W-2 Wage/UBIA Limit
Deduction limited to GREATER of:
- 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, OR
- 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of UBIA of qualified property

### QBI Optimization Strategies
1. **Stay below threshold**: Maximize retirement contributions to push taxable income below
2. **Above threshold (non-SSTB)**: Pay sufficient W-2 wages, acquire qualified property
3. **SSTB**: Use retirement plans and other deductions to stay in/below phase-out
4. **Aggregate businesses**: Election under Reg. 1.199A-4 if beneficial
5. **S-Corp interaction**: Higher salary = lower QBI (but lower SE tax) — optimize both

## Depreciation Strategies (Post-OBBBA)

### Section 179 Expensing
- **2025 limit**: $2,500,000 (OBBBA increased from $1,250,000)
- **Phase-out threshold**: $4,000,000 (2025)
- **2026 limit**: $2,550,000, phase-out at $6,650,000
- Dollar-for-dollar phase-out above threshold
- **Cannot create a business loss** (limited to taxable income from all active businesses)
- Unused amounts carry forward

**Qualifying property**: Tangible personal property, off-the-shelf software, qualified
improvement property, HVAC, roofs, fire protection, security systems

### Bonus Depreciation
- **100% permanently restored** (OBBBA) for property acquired after January 19, 2025
- Previous phase-down (pre-OBBBA): 80% (2023), 60% (2024), 40% (2025), 20% (2026), 0% (2027)
- **NOW**: 100% permanent
- Applies to new AND used property (if new to the taxpayer)
- **CAN create a business loss** (unlike Section 179)
- Applies to most tangible property with recovery period of 20 years or less

### Strategy: Section 179 vs Bonus Depreciation

| Factor | Section 179 | Bonus Depreciation |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Amount control | Can elect partial amount | All or nothing per class |
| Loss creation | Cannot create loss | Can create loss |
| Property types | Broader (some real property) | Narrower (≤20 year recovery) |
| Used property | Yes | Yes (post-TCJA) |
| Order | Applied first | Applied to remaining basis |

**Best practice**: Use Section 179 first for control, then bonus depreciation for the rest.

### Heavy Vehicle Strategy (GVWR > 6,000 lbs)
- Exempt from luxury auto depreciation limits
- Section 179 limit for SUVs: $30,500 (2025)
- Plus 100% bonus depreciation on remaining basis
- Popular vehicles: Ford F-150/250, Chevy Silverado, GMC Sierra, Toyota Land Cruiser,
  BMW X5/X7, Mercedes GLS, Cadillac Escalade, Tesla Model X

### Luxury Auto Limits (GVWR ≤ 6,000 lbs, 2025 with bonus)
- Year 1: $20,400
- Year 2: $19,800
- Year 3: $11,900
- Year 4+: $7,160/year

### Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)
- 15-year recovery period
- Eligible for 100% bonus depreciation (permanent under OBBBA)
- Includes: interior improvements to nonresidential buildings (not enlargement, elevator/escalator, or internal structural framework)

## SALT Deduction Optimization

### New Cap (OBBBA, 2025-2029)

| Year | Cap | Phase-down AGI | Floor |
|------|-----|----------------|-------|
| 2025 | $40,000 | $500,000 | $10,000 |
| 2026 | $40,400 | $505,000 | $10,000 |
| 2027 | $40,800 | $510,000 | $10,000 |
| 2028 | $41,200 | $515,000 | $10,000 |
| 2029 | $41,600 | $520,000 | $10,000 |
| 2030+ | Reverts to $10,000 | N/A | N/A |

Phase-down: Reduced by 30% for each dollar over threshold, cannot go below $10,000

### IL PTE Tax as SALT Workaround
- PTE tax paid at entity level = business deduction (NOT subject to SALT cap)
- Even with $40K cap, PTE valuable when:
  - Total SALT exceeds cap
  - Want to take standard deduction + PTE deduction
  - Want to reduce SE income for partners
- **Caution**: PTE deduction reduces federal QBI (affects 199A deduction)

### SALT Optimization Checklist
1. Calculate total SALT (state income + property + local taxes)
2. If total SALT < $40,000: No PTE election needed for SALT purposes
3. If total SALT > $40,000: Consider PTE election for the excess
4. If AGI > $500,000: Calculate phase-down impact
5. Compare: standard deduction vs. itemized with capped SALT

## Charitable Giving Strategies

### OBBBA Changes (Effective 2026+)
- **New floor**: Charitable deduction only for giving exceeding **0.5% of AGI**
- Tax benefit of itemized deductions capped at $0.35 per $1 (vs $0.37)
- **Action**: Maximize charitable giving in 2025 before floor takes effect

### Bunching Strategy
1. Consolidate 2-3 years of charitable giving into one year
2. Exceeds standard deduction threshold in the "bunching" year
3. Take standard deduction in off-years
4. Works best near the standard deduction threshold

**2025 standard deduction**: $15,750 (single), $31,500 (MFJ)
**2026 standard deduction**: $16,100 (single), $32,200 (MFJ)

### Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)
- Contribute assets to DAF → receive **immediate** tax deduction
- Invest assets tax-free within the DAF
- Recommend grants to charities over time (no deadline)
- Perfect for bunching: large contribution to DAF in one year, distribute over years
- Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, Vanguard Charitable — no minimum at major providers

### Appreciated Stock Donations
- Donate long-term appreciated stock directly to charity or DAF
- Deduct **fair market value** (no capital gains tax on appreciation)
- Deductible up to 30% of AGI (vs 60% for cash)
- **Double tax benefit**: avoid capital gains AND get the deduction

**Example**: Stock purchased at $5,000, now worth $20,000
- If sold: $15,000 gain x 23.8% (LTCG + NIIT) = $3,570 tax, then donate $20,000
- If donated directly: $0 tax on gain + $20,000 deduction. Save $3,570 + deduction value

### Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)
- IRA owners age 70.5+ can distribute directly to charity
- Up to **$105,000** per person per year (2024, indexed)
- Counts toward RMD
- **Excluded from AGI** (better than a deduction)
- Cannot go to DAF or private foundation

### Deduction Limits by Contribution Type

| Type | Limit (% of AGI) |
|------|------------------|
| Cash to public charities | 60% |
| Appreciated property to public charities | 30% |
| Cash to private foundations | 30% |
| Appreciated property to private foundations | 20% |
| Excess carries forward 5 years | |

## Tax Loss Harvesting

### Securities (Stocks, ETFs, Mutual Funds)
- **Wash sale rule**: Cannot deduct loss if "substantially identical" security purchased within 30 days before or after
- Applies across: taxable accounts, IRAs, spouse's accounts
- **Strategy**: Replace with similar (not identical) fund — e.g., swap S&P 500 ETF for total market ETF
- Capital losses offset capital gains dollar-for-dollar
- Excess losses offset up to **$3,000 of ordinary income** per year
- Unused losses carry forward indefinitely

### Cryptocurrency
- Wash sale rule does **NOT** currently apply to directly-held crypto
- Can sell at a loss and immediately repurchase the same cryptocurrency
- **Crypto ETFs ARE securities** — wash sale rule applies
- Expect this loophole to close eventually (legislative proposals pending)
- Starting 2026: brokers report on Form 1099-DA

### Year-End Harvesting Checklist
1. Review portfolio for unrealized losses
2. Identify positions to sell for tax losses
3. Replace with similar (not identical) investments to maintain allocation
4. Track wash sale windows (30 days before and after)
5. Net short-term losses against short-term gains first (higher value)
6. Apply remaining losses against long-term gains
7. Claim up to $3,000 against ordinary income

## Child Tax Credit & Dependent Credits (OBBBA)

### Child Tax Credit (CTC)
- **$2,200 per qualifying child** under 17 (OBBBA increased from $2,000)
- Phase-out: $200,000 (single) / $400,000 (MFJ)
- Reduces by $50 per $1,000 over threshold

### Additional Child Tax Credit (ACTC)
- Refundable up to **$1,700 per child**
- Must have earned income of at least $2,500
- 15% of earned income over $2,500

### Child and Dependent Care Credit
- Up to $3,000 expenses (one child), $6,000 (two+)
- Credit rate: 20-35% depending on AGI
- Max credit: $1,050 (one) or $2,100 (two+) for most taxpayers

### Other Dependents Credit
- $500 per qualifying dependent not eligible for CTC
- Includes children 17+, parents, other dependents

### Trump Accounts (OBBBA — New, effective July 4, 2026)
- $1,000 one-time federal contribution per eligible child
- Individual contributions up to $5,000/year
- Employer contributions up to $2,500/year (tax-free to employee)
- Must invest in U.S. stock index funds
- Cannot withdraw before age 18

## 529 Plan (IL Bright Start)

### IL Tax Benefit
- Deduction: $10,000/individual, $20,000/couple per beneficiary per year
- Tax savings: up to $495 (individual) / $990 (couple) per beneficiary at 4.95%

### Qualified Expenses
- Tuition (any level including K-12 up to $10,000/year)
- Room and board (college)
- Books, supplies, computers
- Student loan repayment (lifetime limit $10,000)

### Superfunding
- Contribute up to 5x annual gift exclusion: $95,000 (2025) per beneficiary
- Elect 5-year gift tax treatment on Form 709

### IL 529 Clawback Rule
- If 529 funds are withdrawn for **non-qualified purposes**, Illinois requires **recapture** of the state income tax deduction previously claimed
- The recaptured amount is added back to IL income in the year of the non-qualified withdrawal
- At 4.95% IL rate, a $10,000 non-qualified withdrawal recaptures ~$495 in IL tax
- **Exception**: Rollover to beneficiary's Roth IRA (SECURE 2.0) is NOT a non-qualified withdrawal (as long as rules are met)
- **Strategy**: Change beneficiary to another family member rather than taking non-qualified withdrawal

### Roth IRA Rollover (SECURE 2.0)
- 529 must have been open 15+ years
- Subject to annual Roth IRA contribution limits
- Lifetime cap: $35,000
- Last 5 years of contributions ineligible

## Student Loan Interest Deduction
- **Above-the-line** deduction (no itemizing required)
- Up to **$2,500** per year
- Income phase-out: $75,000-$90,000 (single), $155,000-$185,000 (MFJ)
- Cannot claim if filing MFS
- Must be legally obligated to pay (co-signer can deduct if they pay)
- Loan must have been taken for qualified higher education expenses
- Reported on Form 1098-E from lender

## Cost Segregation Studies
- Reclassify building components from 27.5-year (residential) or 39-year (commercial) to shorter-lived personal property:
  - **5-year**: Carpeting, certain electrical, specialty plumbing, decorative fixtures
  - **7-year**: Office furniture (built-in), specialty HVAC, signs
  - **15-year**: Land improvements (landscaping, parking lots, fencing, sidewalks, drainage), Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)
- With 100% bonus depreciation (permanent post-OBBBA), reclassified components are **fully deductible in year 1**
- **When it makes sense**: Property value $200,000+ (cost of study typically $3,000-$15,000)
- **Lookback studies**: Can be done on properties placed in service in prior years (file Form 3115 for change in accounting method — automatic consent, no amended returns needed)
- **Example**: $500K commercial building, cost seg reclassifies 20-30% to shorter lives = $100K-$150K first-year deduction (vs ~$12,800 under 39-year straight-line)
- Works for: owned buildings, tenant improvements, rental property
- IRS Audit Guide for Cost Segregation is publicly available — studies following it are defensible

## Estimated Tax Optimization

### Safe Harbor (Avoid Penalty)
- **90%** of current year tax, OR
- **100%** of prior year tax (**110%** if AGI > $150,000)

### Strategies
1. **Prior-year safe harbor**: Simplest — pay 110% of last year's tax in 4 equal installments
2. **Annualized income method**: See detailed section below
3. **W-2 catch-up**: Increase W-2 withholding in Q4 (treated as paid evenly throughout year)
4. **Overpayment application**: Apply refund from prior year to estimated taxes
5. **State coordination**: Pay Q4 IL estimated tax in December for federal SALT deduction

### Annualized Income Installment Method (Form 2210, Schedule AI)

**When to use**: Income is significantly uneven throughout the year — common for:
- Freelancers/consultants with lumpy project payments
- Businesses with seasonal revenue (heavy Q4, light Q1)
- Large one-time gains (asset sale, stock option exercise)
- Year-end bonuses or distributions

**How it works**: Instead of assuming income is earned evenly (standard method), this method calculates the required estimated payment based on income actually earned through each period.

**Annualization periods:**

| Period | Income Through | Annualization Factor | Payment Due |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Period 1 | March 31 | x 4 (12/3) | April 15 |
| Period 2 | May 31 | x 2.4 (12/5) | June 15 |
| Period 3 | August 31 | x 1.5 (12/8) | September 15 |
| Period 4 | November 30 | x 1.09 (12/11) | January 15 |

**Step-by-step calculation (for each period):**
1. Calculate actual taxable income earned through the period end date
2. Multiply by the annualization factor to project full-year income
3. Calculate the tax on the annualized income
4. Multiply tax by the applicable percentage: 22.5% (P1), 45% (P2), 67.5% (P3), 90% (P4)
5. Subtract prior required installments
6. The result is the required payment for that period

**Example**: Freelancer earns $20K in Q1, $20K in Q2, $20K in Q3, $140K in Q4 (total $200K)

| Period | Actual Income | Annualized | Approx Tax | Required Cumulative | Payment Due |
|--------|--------------|------------|------------|--------------------|----|
| P1 (thru 3/31) | $20,000 | $80,000 | ~$12,000 | $2,700 (22.5%) | $2,700 |
| P2 (thru 5/31) | $33,333 | $80,000 | ~$12,000 | $5,400 (45%) | $2,700 |
| P3 (thru 8/31) | $60,000 | $90,000 | ~$14,400 | $9,720 (67.5%) | $4,320 |
| P4 (thru 11/30) | $178,182 | $194,380 | ~$38,000 | $34,200 (90%) | $24,480 |

vs. Standard method (even quarterly payments): ~$8,500/quarter from Q1

**Cash flow benefit**: Pay $2,700 in Q1 instead of $8,500. The difference stays in your pocket for 9 months.

**Filing requirement**: Must complete Schedule AI of Form 2210 and attach to your return. Without it, the IRS will calculate the penalty using the standard method.

**Interaction with W-2 withholding**: If you also have W-2 income, withholding is treated as paid evenly throughout the year regardless of when actually withheld. This means increasing W-2 withholding in Q4 (via updated W-4) can retroactively cover Q1-Q3 shortfalls — a powerful catch-up strategy.

**Illinois**: IL estimated tax uses the same quarterly schedule but does NOT have an annualized income method. IL requires even quarterly payments or prior-year safe harbor. Consider paying IL estimates evenly and using the annualized method only for federal.
