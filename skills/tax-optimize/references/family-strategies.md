# Family Tax Strategies — Hiring Children, Hiring Spouse, Dependent Care, Education Savings

## Hiring Children in the Business

### Sole Prop / Partnership (Unincorporated) — FICA Exempt
- Children under 18 employed by sole proprietorship or partnership where **each partner is a parent**: **EXEMPT from Social Security and Medicare taxes (FICA)**
- Also exempt from **FUTA** (federal unemployment) if under 21
- **IRC Section 3121(b)(3)(A)**
- Child can earn up to the standard deduction **tax-free**: **$15,350 (2025)**, **$16,100 (2026)**
- Parents get a **business deduction** for wages paid
- Child can contribute to **Roth IRA** (up to amount of earned income, max $7,000/2025, $7,500/2026)
- Must be **legitimate work at a reasonable wage** — document hours, duties, and pay
- Acceptable work: filing, data entry, cleaning office, social media, website maintenance, inventory, shipping, answering phones, photography
- Pay must be **reasonable for age and work performed**
- Keep **time sheets, job descriptions, and pay records**
- Pay by **check or direct deposit** (not cash) for documentation
- Issue **W-2** to child at year end (no 1099 — they are an employee)

### S-Corp Workaround
- S-Corps are **NOT exempt** from FICA on wages to owner's children
- **Workaround**: Create a sole prop management company owned by the parent that employs the children. The management company contracts with the S-Corp for services.
- The management company (sole prop) pays the children — **FICA exemption applies**
- Must have **economic substance** — the management company should genuinely provide services
- Documentation is critical — written management agreement, invoices, separate bank account

### Age-Based Work Guidelines

| Age | Suitable Tasks | Max Reasonable Hourly Rate |
|-----|---------------|--------------------------|
| 7-12 | Simple filing, shredding, cleaning, sorting | $8-$12/hr |
| 13-15 | Data entry, social media, photography, inventory | $10-$15/hr |
| 16-17 | Customer service, bookkeeping, web maintenance, shipping | $12-$20/hr |

### Example Tax Savings (2025)
- Parent in 32% bracket + 15.3% SE tax
- Pays child $15,000 for legitimate work
- Parent's deduction: $15,000 x (32% + 15.3% SE tax savings) = **~$7,095 saved**
- Child's tax: **$0** (under standard deduction)
- FICA: **$0** (sole prop exemption)
- Child can fund Roth IRA: **$7,000** (grows tax-free forever)
- **Net family tax savings: ~$7,095/year per child**

### Employing Children in Multiple LLCs
- A multi-LLC owner can employ children across **multiple** sole-prop or partnership entities
- Each entity deducts the wages as a business expense
- Each entity issues a separate **W-2** to the child
- FICA exemption applies **per qualifying entity** (each must be sole prop or partnership where all partners are parents)
- The child's **standard deduction** applies once at the individual level across all W-2s combined
- **Aggregate wages must still be reasonable** for the total work performed across all entities
- Maintain separate time tracking, job descriptions, and pay records per entity
- **Example**: Child works 5 hrs/week for LLC-A ($5,000/year) and 5 hrs/week for LLC-B ($5,000/year). Total $10,000 — well under standard deduction, both entities deduct, zero tax to child, zero FICA.

## Hiring Your Spouse

### Section 105 Medical Reimbursement Plan Strategy
- Hire spouse as **bona fide employee** in sole proprietorship
- Establish a **Section 105 Health Reimbursement Plan** through spouse's employment
- The plan reimburses **ALL family medical expenses** (deductibles, copays, dental, vision, prescriptions, etc.) as a **tax-free fringe benefit**
- Business deducts the reimbursements as employee benefit expense
- **IRC Section 105(b)** — amounts received for medical care are excluded from income
- Requirements: spouse must be bona fide employee (real work, reasonable pay, documented hours)
- The plan must cover all employees in the same class (but if spouse is the only employee, no issue)

### Limitations
- Does **NOT** work for S-Corp >2% shareholder-employees (reimbursements are taxable W-2 income)
- Does **NOT** work for partnerships where spouse is a partner
- Works **BEST** for sole proprietorships and single-member LLCs
- ACA compliance: must be integrated with group health plan or structured as QSEHRA/ICHRA
- Cannot be a "sham" — spouse must perform real services

### QSEHRA (Qualified Small Employer HRA)
- For businesses with **fewer than 50 employees** that don't offer group health plan
- **2025 limits**: $6,350 self-only / $12,800 family
- **2026 limits**: ~$6,500 / ~$13,100 (estimated)
- Tax-free to employees if they have minimum essential coverage
- Deductible by employer
- Reduces employee's ACA premium tax credit dollar-for-dollar

### ICHRA (Individual Coverage HRA)
- **No employee count limit**, no contribution limit
- Employees must purchase individual health insurance
- Cannot be offered alongside traditional group health plan to the same class of employees
- More flexible than QSEHRA but more complex to administer

## Dependent Care Strategies

### Dependent Care FSA (DCFSA)
- Pre-tax set-aside: **$5,000/year** ($2,500 if MFS) — 2025
- OBBBA increased to **$7,500/year** starting 2026
- Reduces both income tax **AND** self-employment tax (if through S-Corp W-2)
- **Use it or lose it** (no rollover, but 2.5-month grace period may apply)
- Requires W-2 employment (works with S-Corp salary)
- Qualifying expenses: daycare, preschool, before/after school care, day camp (NOT overnight camp), au pair, nanny
- Child must be under 13

### Child and Dependent Care Credit (Form 2441)
- Up to $3,000 expenses (one child) or $6,000 (two+ children) — **2025**
- **OBBBA enhanced for 2026+**: $6,000 (one) / $12,000 (two+), credit rate up to 50%
- Credit rate: 20-35% based on AGI (most families get 20%)
- **Nonrefundable** (2025), **partially refundable** (2026+ under OBBBA)
- Cannot use same expenses for both FSA and credit (no double-dipping)

### FSA vs Credit Comparison (2025)

| AGI | FSA Savings (est.) | Credit Amount | Winner |
|-----|-------------------|---------------|--------|
| $50,000 | ~$1,125 (22.5%) | $1,200 (one child, 20%) | Credit |
| $100,000 | ~$1,625 (32.5%) | $600 (one child, 20%) | FSA |
| $150,000 | ~$1,875 (37.5%) | $600 (one child, 20%) | FSA |
| $200,000+ | ~$2,050 (41%) | $600 (one child, 20%) | FSA |

For 2026+: Recalculate with $7,500 FSA and enhanced credit (50% rate, higher expense limits)

### Summer Camp / Day Camp
- Day camp qualifies as dependent care expense (both FSA and credit)
- Overnight camp does **NOT** qualify
- Sports camps, art camps, STEM camps — all qualify if primarily custodial (keeping child while parent works)

## Kiddie Tax (Form 8615)

- Applies to children under 19 (or under 24 if full-time student)
- Child's **unearned income** (interest, dividends, capital gains) above threshold taxed at **parent's rate**
- **2025 threshold**: first $1,300 tax-free, next $1,300 at child's rate, above $2,600 at parent's rate
- **2026 threshold**: ~$1,350 / $1,350 / $2,700 (estimated)
- Applies to: custodial accounts (UGMA/UTMA), trust distributions, investment income
- Does **NOT** apply to earned income (wages from employment)
- **Strategy**: keep child's investment income below the threshold, or invest in growth stocks (no dividends until sold)

## Education Savings Beyond 529

### Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA)
- **$2,000/year** per beneficiary (not deductible, but grows tax-free)
- Income phase-out: $190,000-$220,000 MAGI (MFJ)
- Covers K-12 expenses broadly (uniforms, tutoring, computers, supplies) — more flexible than 529
- Must be used by age 30

### Series EE/I Savings Bonds (Education Exclusion)
- Interest is **tax-free** if used for qualified higher education expenses
- Must be purchased by someone age 24+ (parents purchase for children's education)
- **2025 income phase-out**: $106,100-$136,100 (MFJ)
- Bonds must be redeemed in same year expenses are paid

### Gifting Appreciated Stock to Children — Expanded Strategy

#### Gifting Mechanics
- **Carryover basis rule**: Recipient gets the donor's original cost basis (IRC Section 1015)
- **Exception**: If FMV at time of gift is LESS than donor's basis, the recipient's basis for LOSS purposes is FMV (prevents creating artificial losses through gifts)
- **Holding period**: Donor's holding period tacks onto recipient's (so gift of stock held 3 years = long-term capital gain if sold immediately)

#### Step-by-Step Strategy for Multi-LLC Owner with Kids

**Scenario A: Child under 19 (or under 24 if full-time student)**
1. Gift stock with embedded gain of up to $2,600 (2025 kiddie tax threshold)
2. Child sells: first $1,300 of unearned income = tax-free, next $1,300 taxed at child's rate (likely 0% LTCG)
3. Above $2,600 = taxed at parent's rate (kiddie tax applies)
4. Net benefit: up to $2,600 in gains realized at 0% tax rate vs parent's 15-23.8% rate
5. Tax savings: up to $618/year per child (at 23.8% parent rate on $2,600)

**Scenario B: Child age 19+ (not full-time student) or age 24+ (student)**
- Kiddie tax no longer applies
- Child can realize gains taxed entirely at their own rate
- If child has no other income: 0% LTCG rate on gains up to $49,450 (single, 2026)
- Strategy: Gift stock, child sells, realizes up to $49,450 in LTCG at 0% rate
- Tax savings: up to $7,417/year (at 15% parent rate) or $11,769 (at 23.8% with NIIT)

**Scenario C: Gift stock to fund child's Roth IRA**
- Child must have earned income (from family business employment)
- Gift appreciated stock → child sells at low/zero rate → contributes cash to Roth IRA
- Result: appreciated stock liquidated at child's low rate, funds grow tax-free in Roth forever

#### Gift Tax Considerations
- Annual exclusion: $19,000 per recipient (2025) — gift of stock valued at FMV
- If FMV of gifted stock exceeds $19,000: file Form 709, but no gift tax unless lifetime exemption ($13.99M in 2025) is exhausted
- Married couples: gift-splitting allows $38,000 per recipient
- **Warning**: Assignment of income doctrine — do NOT gift stock AFTER a sale has been arranged. The gain must not be "ripe" at the time of gift.

### Family LLC for Wealth Transfer (Advanced)
- Create an LLC (or LP) owned by parents and children
- Parents contribute assets (investments, real estate) to the entity
- Parents gift LLC membership interests to children over time using the **$19,000 annual gift exclusion** (2025)
- **Valuation discounts**: LLC interests are typically valued at a 25-40% discount from the underlying asset value due to:
  - **Lack of marketability** (15-35% discount) — interests can't be sold on a public market
  - **Minority interest** (15-40% discount) — recipient doesn't control the entity
- Combined discount of 25-45% means $19,000 of gifted interest transfers $25,000-$35,000 of underlying asset value
- Children's income from the LLC is taxed at their lower rates (subject to kiddie tax rules)
- **Key case**: Hackl v. Commissioner — gifts must be of "present interest" (not just future) to qualify for annual exclusion
- **Requirements**: Entity must have legitimate business purpose, operating agreement must restrict transfers, regular distributions should be made
- **IL consideration**: IL LLC annual report ($75) applies per entity

### Annual Gift Tax Exclusion Strategy
- **$19,000** per person per recipient in 2025 (indexed for inflation)
- Married couples can give **$38,000** per recipient (gift-splitting, Form 709)
- No limit on number of recipients
- Common recipients: children, grandchildren, trusts, 529 plans
- Direct tuition payments (to the school) and medical payments (to the provider) are **unlimited** and do NOT count against the $19,000 exclusion — IRC Section 2503(e)
- **Strategy**: Pay grandchildren's tuition directly to school + give $19,000 cash = both exclude from gift tax

## Custodial Accounts (UGMA/UTMA)

- Assets are **irrevocable gift** to the child
- Subject to kiddie tax on unearned income (see above)
- Child gains full control at age of majority (18 or 21 depending on state — **IL is 21 for UTMA**)
- Counted as **student's asset** for FAFSA (reduces financial aid by **20%** of value)
- 529 plans are generally preferred (parent-owned, counted as parent asset for FAFSA at **5.64%**)
- Tax benefit: first $1,300 of unearned income tax-free, next $1,300 at child's rate (typically 10%)
- **Strategy**: gift appreciated stock to child, sell in child's account at lower rate (subject to kiddie tax limits)

## Filing Child's Own Return

- If child has earned income, they should **file their own return**
- Standard deduction on earned income: **$15,350 (2025)**, **$16,100 (2026)**
- Even below filing threshold, file to **get refund of any withheld taxes**
- Establishes **tax filing history**
- If child has only earned income below standard deduction: **zero tax liability**
- If child has unearned income: kiddie tax rules apply (see above)
